The basics

Sentences end in verbs. A verb in its default, present-tense form ends in an u うsound.

私わたしは生いきる。
watashi wa ikiru.
I live.

The waは in (1) is a type of word called a particle, which can be attached to the end of another word to mark its grammatical purpose. In this case, watashi wa私わたしは means that the sentence is about 私わたしwatashi (which means "I"). Although it's written with the kana haは, when used as a particle, it's pronounced waわ.

今日きょうは月曜日げつようびだ。
kyou wa getsuyoubi da.
Today is Monday.

This sentence ends with the verb だda, which means "is". Incidentally, this is the only present-tense verb that doesn't end in an u うsound.

To talk about the past, just conjugate the verb; in this case だda becomes だったdatta:

昨日きのうは私わたし の誕生日たんじょうびだった。
kinou wa watashi no tanjoubi datta.
Yesterday was my birthday.

Oops, another particle. のno works exactly like apostrophe-s does in English. Xの no Y means X's Y.

We also need to be able to negate our statements. Here are the negative forms of だ (is) and だった (was).

私わたしは日本人にほんじんじゃない。
watashi wa nihonjin janai
I am not Japanese.

だda becomes じゃないjanai in the negative form. This doesn't follow any regular pattern, so just memorize it.

彼女は犯人じゃなかった。
kanojo wa hannin janakatta
She was not the culprit.

だった becomes じゃなかった when negated; or rather, the present-tense negative じゃない becomes the past-tense negative じゃなかった. As in English, the negative past is often used when rejecting a hypothesis. To summarize:

Present Past
Affirmative だda だったdatta
Negative じゃないjanai じゃなかったjanakatta

Ichidan verbs

There are two main kinds of verbs: ichidan and godan. Ichidan verbs are simpler, so let's deal with those first.

Ichidan verbs always end in "ru", but not all verbs that end in "ru" are ichidan verbs. To conjugate an ichidan verb, you'll replace the final "ru" with something else.

The negative form of an ichidan verb is created by replacing "ru" with "nai". For example, ere's the negative of taberu, to eat:

私わたしは魚を食べない。
watashi wa sakana o tabenai.
I do not eat fish.

Aha, a new particle. を denotes the object of an action, and is pronounced "o" rather than "wo".

Let's form the past tense of an ichidan verb. Replace "ru" with "ta"; in this case, using the verb miru (to see):

私は昨日の番組を見た。
watashi wa kinou no bangumi o mita.
I saw yesterday's TV show.

Remember how じゃない (negative) became じゃなかった (past negative)? Turns out that's a regular pattern. If something ends in ない in the negative form, the negative past is formed by replacing ない with なかった. In the following example we use the verb wasureru -> wasurenai -> wasurenakatta:

私はあの約束を忘れなかった。
watashi wa ano yakusoku o wasurenakatta.
I did not forget that promise.

あの is an adjective that means "that" and can be placed before nouns just like in English. Here's the summary of the conjugations we just learned, again using 食べる as an example:

Present Past
Affirmative 食べるtaberu 食べたtabeta
Negative 食べないtabenai 食べなかったtabenakattaたb

That wraps up the negative, past, and past negative forms of ichidan verbs. We'll get back to godan verbs and more advanced conjugations later.

Directionality and time

私わたしの母ははは東京 とうきょうに行いく。
watashi no haha wa toukyou ni iku.
My mother goes to Tokyo.

Another new particle. In this case にni marks the destination of a verb. It can also do many other things, as we'll see later. By the way, we can also interpret (3) as "my mother will go to Tokyo." Japanese lacks a real future tense, so context is used to tell the difference, as in the following sentence:

俺おれは海賊王かいぞくおうになる！
ore wa kaizokuou ni naru!
I will become the pirate king!

Here に marks the destination of なる (to become), even though it's not a physical place. Also note that we've switched pronouns from 私 (polite, sometimes feminine) to 俺 (rough, masculine). Incidentally, Luffy from One Piece usually phrases his ambitions a bit differently:

海賊王かいぞくおうに俺おれはなる！
kaizokuou ni ore wa naru
I will become the pirate king!

We've flipped the order of the first two terms, which is legal in Japanese. You can put words marked by particles pretty much anywhere as long as the verb remains at the end. Word function is determined by the associated particle(s) and not by position in the sentence.
