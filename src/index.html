Japanese grammar

How fast can you learn Japanese grammar? Let's find out.

This is a non-standard tutorial, where I've simplified and combined concepts in order to minimize the amount of information you need to digest.

You can switch between rōmaji and Japanese script using the toggle below (or by pressing the r and j keys), but if you're studying seriously you should definitely learn to read kana. I've provided furigana (kana reading aid) everywherer, so you don't need to know any kanji.

If you don't know a word, just paste it into jisho.

Affirmative and negative statements

Sentences end in verbs. A verb in its default, present-tense form ends in an u うsound.

私わたしは生いきる。
watashi wa ikiru.
I live.

waは is a type of word called a particle, which can be attached to the end of another word to mark its grammatical purpose. Although it's written with the kana haは, when used as a particle, it's pronounced waわ. In this case, watashi wa私わたしは means that the sentence is about 私わたしwatashi (the standard first-person pronoun; I).

今日きょうは月曜日げつようびだ。
kyou wa getsuyoubi da.
Today is Monday.

This sentence ends with the verb だda, which means "is". Incidentally, this is the only present-tense verb that doesn't end in an u うsound.

To talk about the past, just conjugate the verb; in this case だda becomes だったdatta:

昨日きのうは私わたし の誕生日たんじょうびだった。
kinou wa watashi no tanjoubi datta.
Yesterday was my birthday.

Oops, another particle. のno works exactly like apostrophe-s does in English. Xの no Y means X's Y.

We also need to be able to negate our statements. Here are the negative forms of だda (is) and だったdatta (was).

私わたしは日本人にほんじんじゃない。
watashi wa nihonjin janai.
I am not Japanese.

だda becomes じゃないjanai in the negative form. This doesn't follow any regular pattern, so just memorize it.

彼女は犯人じゃなかった。
kanojo wa hannin janakatta.
She was not the culprit.

だったdatta becomes じゃなかったjanakatta when negated; or rather, the present-tense negative じゃないjanai becomes the past-tense negative じゃなかったjanakatta. As in English, the negative past is often used when rejecting a hypothesis. To summarize:

Present Past
Affirmative だda だったdatta
Negative じゃないjanai じゃなかったjanakatta

Ichidan verbs

There are two main kinds of verbs: ichidan and godan. Ichidan verbs are simpler, so let's deal with those first.

Ichidan verbs always end in ruる, but not all verbs that end in ruる are ichidan verbs. To conjugate an ichidan verb, you'll replace the final ruる with something else.

The negative form of an ichidan verb is created by replacing ruる with naiない. For example, here's the negative of 食たべるtaberu, to eat:

私わたしは魚さかなを食 たべない。
watashi wa sakana o tabenai.
I do not eat fish.

Aha, a new particle. を marks the object of an action, and is pronounced "o" rather than "wo".

Let's form the past tense of an ichidan verb. Replace ruる with taた; in this case, using the verb miru見み る (to see):

私わたしは昨日きのうの番組 ばんぐみ を見みた。
watashi wa kinou no bangumi o mita.
I saw yesterday's TV show.

Remember how じゃないjanai (negative) became じゃなかったjanakatta (past negative)? Turns out that's a regular pattern. If something ends in naiない in the negative form, the negative past is formed by replacing naiない with nakattaなかった. In the following example we use the verb wasureru -> wasurenai -> wasurenakatta:

私わたしはあの約束を忘れなかった。
watashi wa ano yakusoku o wasurenakatta.
I did not forget that promise.

あの is an adjective that means "that" and can be placed before nouns just like in English.

Here's the summary of the conjugations we just learned, again using 食た べるtaberu (to eat) as an example:

Present Past
Affirmative 食たべるtaberu 食たべたtabeta
Negative 食たべないtabenai 食たべなかったtabenakatta

You'll see this pattern of four basic forms repeatedly across different situations and parts of speech.

Adjectives

There are two main kinds of adjectives. In this section we'll cover い-adjectives, which end in い. These can be placed directly before a noun, just like in English:

すごい物ものを見た。
sugoi mono o mita.
I saw something incredible.

Here すごい (incredible, amazing) describes 物 (thing). By the way, Japanese allows you to leave out the topic (in this case, 私は) if it's implied by context, as it is above. In fact, this way of speaking is the most common, so I'm going to use it frequently from now on.

It's also possible to use i-adjectives with は, as follows:

私の彼女はとても可愛い。
watashi no kanojo wa totemo kawaii.
My girlfriend is very cute.

とても means "very", and you can stick it before adjectives without thinking too hard. The important thing to note here is that we didn't add だ (is) to the end of the sentence. You cannot use だ with い-adjectives; in some sense the "is" is implied.

i-adjectives can also be conjugated:

風呂は温かくない。
furo wa atatakakunai.
The bath is not warm.

The adjective 温かい means pleasantly warm. We swap out the last い for くない to get the negative 温かくない (bit of a mouthful). There's also a past tense:

昨日の天気はとても寒かった。
kinou no tenki wa totemo samukatta.
Yesterday's weather was very cold.

In the past tense, い becomes かった. The past negative follows the usual rule for things ending in ない, so I'll skip the example and just give you the conjugation table for 可愛い (cute):

Present Past
Affirmative かわいいkawaii かわいかったkawaikatta
Negative かわいくないkawaikunai かわいくなかったkawaikunakatta

And of course these conjugated forms can be placed before nouns just like the base form:

。
kinou no tenki wa totemo samukatta.
Yesterday's weather was very cold.

Verbs II: Godan

Directionality and time

私わたしの母はは は東京 とうきょうに行いく。
watashi no haha wa toukyou ni iku.
My mother goes to Tokyo.

Another new particle. In this case にni marks the destination of a verb. It can also do many other things, as we'll see later. By the way, we can also interpret (3) as "my mother will go to Tokyo." Japanese lacks a real future tense, so context is used to tell the difference, as in the following sentence:

俺おれは海賊王かいぞくおう になる！
ore wa kaizokuou ni naru!
I will become the pirate king!

Here に marks the destination of なる (to become), even though it's not a physical place. Also note that we've switched pronouns from 私 (polite, sometimes feminine) to 俺 (casual, masculine). Incidentally, Luffy from One Piece usually phrases his ambitions a bit differently:

海賊王かいぞくおうに俺おれ はなる！
kaizokuou ni ore wa naru
I will become the pirate king!

We've flipped the order of the first two terms, which is legal in Japanese. You can put words marked by particles pretty much anywhere as long as the verb remains at the end. Word function is determined by the associated particle(s) and not by position in the sentence.
